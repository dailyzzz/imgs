However, argument misinterprets what time-lapse photos show and misunderstands orbital mechanics: 1) PERSPECTIVE AND VIEWING ANGLE: All four photos show launches viewed from ground relatively close to launch site (within tens of miles). Observer sees rocket ascend, then disappear over horizon or due to distance, creating appearance of "coming back down" in frame. What looks like descent is actually rocket continuing horizontal acceleration while vertical component decreases, combined with Earth's curvature taking rocket over horizon and out of view. 2) MULTI-STAGE ROCKETS: Modern orbital rockets use staging - first stage burns for few minutes lifting rocket through dense atmosphere, then separates and falls back to Earth (sometimes landing on droneships for reuse - SpaceX Falcon 9). Second stage continues firing, delivering payload to orbit. Time-lapse showing "ascending then descending" trail might show first stage separation and return while second stage (no longer visible/creating exhaust trail) continues to orbit. 3) EXPOSURE TIME: Long exposures capture several minutes of flight. During this time, rocket transitions from vertical ascent to gravity turn - gradually pitching over toward horizontal to gain orbital velocity. Orbital insertion requires horizontal velocity (17,500 mph for low Earth orbit), not vertical altitude. Rocket appears to "come back down" because it's transitioning to horizontal flight path (orbital trajectory is parallel to Earth's surface), not because it's falling. 4) LIGHTING: Rocket exhaust visible when illuminated - during night launch or twilight when sun angle allows. Once rocket reaches high altitude or moves away from sun angle, exhaust trail becomes invisible even though rocket continues firing. What appears as trail "ending" or "coming down" is actually trail becoming invisible while rocket continues upward/horizontally to orbit. 5) DISTANCE AND RESOLUTION: After few minutes of flight, rocket is dozens or hundreds of miles away from camera, making it too small/dim to see, even if still ascending. Trail "ends" not because rocket stopped but because it's too far away to photograph. Evidence rockets reach space: Satellites remain in orbit for years (GPS, communications, weather, ISS), ground stations track satellites showing consistent orbital paths, amateur radio operators communicate with ISS passing overhead, satellite passes visible to naked eye at predictable times (ISS, Starlink trains, Iridium flares), astronauts from multiple nations report weightlessness and Earth views from orbit, telemetry data from missions shows orbital velocities and altitudes, spent rocket stages tracked in orbit by space surveillance networks, some first stages recovered and reused (SpaceX) confirming staging works as described. Parabolic appearance is optical illusion of perspective: Ballistic trajectory (object thrown upward) is parabola in vertical plane. Orbital trajectory is ellipse (or circle) in horizontal plane around Earth. From ground observer's perspective, rocket transitioning from vertical ascent to horizontal orbital insertion appears parabolic even though actual trajectory is orbital ellipse. Time-lapse photos can't distinguish between ballistic arc and orbital trajectory without altitude/velocity data. Meme suggests: "images prove rockets come back down." Reality: images show partial trajectory from one viewing angle during first minutes of multi-stage launch. Doesn't show: orbital insertion burn, second stage performance, satellite deployment, orbital mechanics. Comparing rocket launch to ballistic trajectory misses fundamental difference: ballistic object has single velocity impulse (thrown/fired), orbital rocket continuously thrusts for 8-10 minutes, accelerating to orbital velocity. Four photos show different missions (different locations, times, rocket types) but meme treats them as representative of all launches, ignoring thousands of successful orbital missions documented over 60+ years of space age.
